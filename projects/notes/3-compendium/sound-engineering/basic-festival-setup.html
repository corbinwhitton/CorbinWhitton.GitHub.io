Scribble Strip Key

Colors: Input

  • Green: Vocalist (High Chance of Feedback)
  • Red: Instrument (Medium Chance of Feedback)
  • Yellow: Originating from a computer (No Chance of feedback)
  • Purple: Speaker with a headset microphone (Important, should take priority over Teal, high chance of feedback)
  • Teal: Floating speaker with a handheld microphone (High Chance of Feedback)
  • Black: Unused/Standby

Colors: Busses/Matrices

  • Yellow: Originating from a computer
  • Black: Unused/Standby
  • Orange: Destined to Be a direct output
  • Green: Meant for monitoring

Colors: DCA Groups

  • White: Multiple groups
  • Black: Unused/Standby
  • Other: Follows other coloring standards.

Routing

  • All IEM stage monitors should be tapped pre-mute, post-gain.
  • All stage wedge monitors should be tapped post-mute, pre-eq.

Timeline

  • Plan on arriving early enough to setup the stage for the performers so sound-check can start as soon as they arrive. Ensure the following items are done:
    • Sound system is on, and ready (Any outputs should be muted at this point)
    • Stage is laid out as specified in the design plan
    • Each performer has any DI boxes, cables, or power that they need to perform.
    • Take a note how everything is plugged into the system, and if any routing changes needed to happen (for example, if the guitarist is plugged into 16, and was rerouted to appear in channel 5)
    • All monitoring equipment is connected, and in working order.
    • Gaff down any cords that may become a tripping hazard.
    • If there are central crew coms, verify that everyone can hear you clearly, and you can hear coordinators clearly.
    • Make a note on any borrowed equipment and who it belongs to.
    • Unmute main speakers last, ensuring each input is muted before sound check.
  • Sound check: Ensure each instrument and input is working and can be heard all the way through its desired output. This includes any media or lighting sends.
  • Pre-event planning: While the performers get ready for the event, ensure EQs, dynamics, and effects sound well. Take notes on the following items:
    • Timeline and order of the events
    • Important cues for each segment of the event
    • If there's music or speaking, take notes on who's leading
    • If additional dynamics are needed, such as changing out EQs, effects, or fader variations, take a note of what and when.
    • Run through an overview of your notes with your Point-of-contact.
    • Understand if any digital control systems are set up and how they'll affect you (such as MIDI, OSC, USB, HTTP APIs, etc)
    • Ensure any recording devices are armed and ready to record
    • Ensure all stage monitors are at an acceptable level while minimizing the chance of feedback.
    • Ensure to check with ALL performers if they have any comments, questions, or concerns regarding their sound and incorporate criticism early
    • Prepare all speaker microphones with fresh batteries, and ensure speakers have been briefed on how to use a microphone, what to do if it's muted, how to unmute, etc (Though, locking mics is preferred). Ensure any speakers wearing a headset microphone has it on correctly (not bending the wires too much), and the tip of the lavalier microphone is at the corner of their mouth, but not touching their face; make sure they know not to touch it.
    • Before the actual event, check battery levels of all microphones, and ensure that they'll last the entire performance.
    • If you're using a digital mixer, ensure all scene configurations have been saved, backed up internally (and externally if possible).
    • Have a quick briefing with your backstage team to see if there's anything that you missed in your notes.
  • Actual Event: While the event happens, ensure any necessary recordings are started
  • Post-event
    • Immediately mute all inputs that are not desired to be heard.
    • Ensure any recordings have been stopped and delivered to the POC or designee.
    • Debrief with the POC to see if there was anything that could have gone better or that worked well.
    • Debrief with the behind-the-stage team and see if there was anything that could have gone better or that worked well.
    • Strike the stage, ensuring all sound equipment is returned how you found it or where it's supposed to go.
    • Ensure all borrowed equipment is returned promptly to its owner.
    • Finally, fade out any remaining audio, ending with the mains muted. Then turn off the sound system in the proper order.
    • Do one final walk-through to ensure everything's put away, that all borrowed items are returned, that everything's off
    • Collect all your things including monitoring equipment, any microphones or cables lent for the event.
