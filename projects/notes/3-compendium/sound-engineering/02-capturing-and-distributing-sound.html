Before you start

Having a game plan is critical to a performance’s success. Having a plan alleviates stress, and allows for greater creativity since the bare necessities are already figured out.

Some good starting questions and responses for building a plan are as follows:

  • Who is performing? What inputs will they need access to? - Input Chart
  • What technology are the performers using? Do I need DI boxes, microphones, MIDI network access? - Tech List
  • Where is the sound going? Do they need it recorded or broadcasted? - Routing & Mixing
  • How much time do the performers need to prepare? When will they be arriving? When are they doing a line test or practicing? - Schedule

Setting Up the Stage for a Live Performance

  1. Prepare the space: Ensure that the stage area is clean and free of any obstacles. Move any furniture, props, or other items that may be in the way.
  2. Set up the sound system: Assemble the sound system components, including the speakers, mixer, and any other necessary equipment. Connect all cables and make sure that everything is properly powered on.
  3. Test the sound system: Perform a sound check to ensure that all components of the system are working properly. Make any necessary adjustments to volume levels or other settings.
  4. Set up the stage lighting: Assemble any stage lighting fixtures, including stands, lights, and cables. Connect the lights to power sources and make sure that they are working properly.
  5. Set up the backdrop: Unroll and hang the backdrop behind the stage. Make sure that it is properly positioned and secured in place.
  6. Arrange the stage: Place any necessary props or furniture on the stage. Make sure that everything is properly positioned and arranged in a way that is aesthetically pleasing.
  7. Check the slideshow: Start the slideshow and make sure that all images, videos and texts are being displayed correctly. Ensure that the display is working properly and make any necessary adjustments.
  8. Check the stage: Walk around the stage to make sure that everything is in place and working properly. Make any necessary adjustments or corrections.
  9. Prepare the music stand: Set up the music stand and make sure that it is in the correct position for the musicians.
  10. Prepare any other necessary equipment: Set up any other necessary equipment, such as microphones, instruments, or music stands. Make sure that everything is in its proper place and ready to use.
  11. Greet the congregation: Welcome the congregation as they arrive and help them find their seats. Make sure that everyone is comfortable and that the stage is ready for the performance.

This guide should provide a basic overview of the steps involved in setting up a stage for a church on Sunday mornings. It may need to be adapted or modified to fit the specific needs and requirements of your church or venue.

Microphones

There are three different types of microphones that are used in live performances. Each have their own uses, strengths, and weaknesses.

Dynamic microphones are the most commonly used type of microphone for live performances, and are known for their robustness and durability. They work by converting sound waves into electrical signals using a diaphragm and a coil of wire that moves within a magnetic field. Dynamic microphones are often less sensitive than other types of microphones, which makes them ideal for use in high-volume settings, as they are less likely to distort or clip the input signal.

Condenser microphones, on the other hand, use a thin metal diaphragm, charged with an electrical voltage, that vibrates when it is exposed to sound waves. This vibration changes the electrical capacitance of the diaphragm, which is then converted into an electrical signal. Condenser microphones are more sensitive than dynamic microphones and are often used in studios and other professional recording environments where high levels of accuracy and detail are required. Since condenser microphones are charged with an electrical voltage, external power is needed to make them function, known as Phantom Power, also denoted as +48v, which injects the power into the XLR cable.

A compressor microphone is a type of condenser microphone that has an internal compressor built-in. The compressor circuit reduces the dynamic range of the microphone, which helps to reduce the volume of loud sounds and increase the volume of soft sounds. This makes compressor microphones ideal for use in situations where there are significant variations in the volume of the sound being captured, as it helps to ensure that the output signal is consistent and balanced. Since compressor microphones are condenser microphones, external power is needed to make them function, known as Phantom Power, also denoted as +48v, which injects the power into the XLR cable.

Microphone Placement

  • Place microphones close to the source: Placing microphones close to the source of sound (e.g. an instrument or singer)
  • Use directional microphones: Directional microphones (e.g. cardioid, supercardioid, and hypercardioid) can be used to capture sound from a specific direction and rejecting sound from other directions.
  • Avoid pointing multiple microphones at the same source: Pointing multiple microphones at the same source can lead to phase cancellations and the comb filtering effect. To avoid this, try to use only one microphone or place multiple microphones at different angles to the source. If you are amplifying a choir, and have no choice but to use multiple microphones, see the 05 - Routing, and Advanced Techniques > 3 1 Rule.
  • Use microphone isolation techniques: Microphone isolation techniques (e.g. gobos, screens, and foam shields) can help to reduce the pickup of sound from other sources
  • Experiment with microphone placement: The best microphone placement will vary depending on the specific situation, so it's important to experiment with different microphone placement techniques to find the best results. Pay attention to factors such as distance, angle, and orientation to the source, and be prepared to make adjustments as needed.
  • If you’re struggling picking up the deeper, richer, low ends of a sound using a microphone, consider moving the microphone much closer to the source. As low-frequency sounds are dispersed much more easily.

Cables

There are a lot of different mediums that sound can be moved from one place to another. The most used audio cables, and likely, the ones you’re most familiar with is RCA, XLR, TS/TRS, and USB cables. There are two different types of cables, digital and analog. Analog cables carry the actual noise signal, while digital cables carry bits (1s or 0s) that approximate and represent the audio signal. When mixing and matching audio cables, converters such as Direct Injection (DI) boxes often need to be used to convert, normalize, balance, and translate one signal to another.

Balanced or Unbalanced? Which should I choose?

For longer cable runs, especially parallel cables, balanced cables are far superior. This is because balanced cables have three wires, one wire carries the signal, one wire carries the same signal but with its polarity reversed so the signals cancel out (like adding +4 and -4 equals 0), and a ground wire to help shield against interference. Audio gear would just need to flip the polarity of one of the signal wires to get sound.

Unbalanced cables, on the other hand have just two wires, a signal wire that carries the sound, and a ground wire. With only one signal wire, an unbalanced cable is far more susceptible to interference (can be a hum or static). Unbalanced cables are not a good idea if multiple cables are going to be ran close together.

You can use a DI (direct injection) box to convert an unbalanced cable like a TS cable to a balanced cable like XLR

Pictured is a Direct Injection (DI) box. This is a great way to connect instruments with a TS cable to a soundboard using an XLR cable. Some DI boxes have a Ground/Lift button that disconnects pin 1 from the XLR cabel. This is useful for connecting any equipment that already has a ground such as a guitar amplifier that's different than what the XLR cable eventually plugs in to. Static noise can occur if there's a different "common ground" than what's processing the signal. If you don't what to use just follow this simple rule, if the source of the signal is plugged in using 2 prongs or isn't powered with AC power (such as with batteries), don't use the Ground/Lift. However, if the source of the signal is plugged in using 3 prongs, use the Ground/Lift for a cleaner sound.

You can also decrease the output signal of a DI box by using the pad which decreases the "loudness" of the signal by 15dBA or sometimes 30dBA, which is why it makes it so versatile. A DI box can be used for TONS of different things, and can handle line level, instrument level, or mic level. Line level is super loud, instrument level is medium loud, and mic level is a bit quieter.

Some DI boxes can also have a "Thru" output, which allows an unprocessed signal to be fed in to an external device such as an effects rack or an amp, which can allow a guitarist to monitor themselves on the stage. Nifty.

Tearing Down (Striking) the Stage

For the most part, we try to just "reset" the stage to how we found it. While that's beind done, there's some important things to consider while putting things away.

  1. If the sound system is running, how will unplugging things affect the sound. Active cables such as TS cables, and even XLR cables to an extent will make make an audible pop when plugged in or unplugged. And if you're powering off something, make sure it's muted.
  2. If a cable is being wound up, am I doing it in a way so that it minimizes knots, and preserves the integrity of the cable? Most audio cables should always be wound up using the over/under method, if you don't know how, check out this video, to sum it up, you're alternating twists so that if the net twists is zero so it can be can be quickly thrown across a stage to make quick point-to-point connections. TS and TRS cables are okay to be over/under wrapped, but is better to "follow the flow" of the cable to minimize twists at all, and they're generally short enough to manage by hand. Any optical cables shouldn't ever be twisted, and is recommended to "follow the flow" of the cable. Some venues prefer specific ways to wrap extension cables, and it's a good idea to follow the desired method, as such, you should at least know how to wrap up cables in a variety of different ways such as daisy-chaining.
  3. Make sure any equipment you borrow or lend out gets returned to its proper owner, there's nothing more frustrating than searching for something and it's not there.
