Routing

In a digital mixer, routing refers to the process of sending audio signals from one place to another within the mixer. The routing capabilities of a digital mixer can be quite complex and flexible, allowing for a wide range of signal processing and mixing options.

  • Busses: Busses are virtual pathways within a digital mixer that allow audio signals to be combined and processed together. For example, you might use a bus to combine several channels of audio into a single stereo mix. Busses can be assigned to outputs such as speakers or headphones, or to processing tools such as effects.
  • Matrices: Matrices are similar to busses, but allow for more complex signal routing options. With a matrix, you can route any channel or bus to any output or processing tool within the mixer. This can be useful for creating custom monitor mixes or for routing signals to multiple processing tools.
  • DCAs (Digitally Controlled Amplifiers): DCAs are a type of group processing that allow you to control the level of multiple channels or busses with a single control. For example, you might use a DCA to control the level of all drum channels in a mix. DCAs can be especially useful in live sound reinforcement, where you need to make quick and accurate level adjustments to multiple channels at once.

In summary, routing in a digital mixer involves the assignment of audio signals to various pathways within the mixer, allowing you to control and process the signals as needed. Busses, matrices, and DCAs are some of the tools that can be used to control and manipulate the routing of audio signals in a digital mixer.

Psycho-acoustics

Okay, this one's a bit advanced, but if you understand a few concepts about Psycho-acoustics (How music affects human psychology), you can easily make music that people can connect with.

The Importance of Lower Frequencies

If you want to make music that people connect with, the first thing you need to know is how lower frequencies affect how people perceive music. If you've looked at the average human's frequency response curve, there's a sharp drop off below anything under 100hz. For the most part, anything under that threshold starts to become "percusive", it's sound that you can feel more than hear. Because of that, you can curate sound that feels like it has energy, it can be used to evoke strong emotional feelings in humans. Mid-bass (about 50-80hz) feels punchy, and can be felt in your head. Low-bass (about 30-50hz) can be felt in the chest and is described as "fullness" or as the "bass body". Ultra-low-bass (<25hz) creates more of a pulsating feeling, and is tough to achieve with conventional sub-woofers making some resort to rotary sub-woofers.

Advanced Techniques

  1. Microphone placement: Experiment with different microphone placement techniques to find the best sound for each instrument or performer. Pay attention to factors such as distance, angle, and orientation to the source.

  2. Dynamic range compression: Use dynamic range compression to control the difference between the loudest and softest sounds in a mix. This can help to prevent distortion and ensure that the audio remains at a consistent level.

  3. Equalization: Use equalization (EQ) to shape the tone of individual sounds in a mix. For example, you can use high-pass filters to remove low-frequency rumble or boost high frequencies to add clarity.

  4. Reverb: Add reverb to create a sense of space and depth in a mix. Experiment with different reverb types and settings to find the best sound for each situation.

  5. Delay: Use delay to create a sense of spaciousness and depth in a mix. Experiment with different delay times and feedback levels to find the best sound for each situation.

  6. Automation: Use automation to make precise, repeatable changes to the levels and settings of individual sounds in a mix. For example, you can use automation to gradually bring up the volume of a guitar solo or fade out a vocal track.

  7. Stereo imaging: Use stereo imaging techniques to create a wider, more spacious soundstage. Experiment with panning, delay, and reverb to find the best sound for each situation.

  8. Sidechain compression: Use sidechain compression to control the level of one sound in relation to another. For example, you can use sidechain compression to reduce the volume of a bass line whenever a kick drum hits.

  9. Don’t be afraid to experiment. You will only succeed by the same measure you’re willing to fail.

    200Hz (Mud) – Too much can cause the track or the mix to sound muddy or boomy, while not enough of it can make it sound thin. It’s a fine line, but many times mixers err on the side of too much and end up with a track that’s too thick that clutters up the mix.

    300 to 500Hz (Boxy) – Too much of this frequency area results in the dreaded “boxiness” sound, or if you’re listening to a floor tom or kick, the “beach ball” effect. It’s also a spot that some less expensive microphones (especially dynamics) tend to emphasize, which is why many mixers almost automatically cut a a few dB of this area out of the kick drum during the mix.

    800Hz (Walmart) – Too much in this area results in what’s sometimes known as the “Walmart” sound, meaning that it sounds like a cheap stereo purchased in a department store. Try it for yourself – get a cheap pair of computer speakers and you’ll find that 800Hz is what you’ll mostly hear. Obviously, too much of this frequency range is not a good thing.

    1k to 1.5kHz (Nasal) – This is the nasal range of the frequency spectrum and, as the name suggests, too much results in a vocalist that sounds like she’s singing through her nose. Once again this is primarily a microphone problem in that it’s poorly matched to the vocalist, but notching a bit out during the mix can fix it.

    4kHz to 6kHz (Presence) – This frequency range is frequently underutilized during the mix, resulting in a track that lacks definition. Without it, things tend to sound dull, but too much can make the track sound thin or, in the case of a vocal, sibilant.

    10kHz+ (Air) – Another widely overlooked frequency band, this provides clarity and adds a certain “realness” to the track. Many vintage mics have a lot of the air frequencies, which is why we prize them for their sound. The Maag Audio EQ4P has a special “Air Band” designed to provide those frequencies with a minimum of phase shift, but you can dial it in other equalizers as well.

3:1 Rule

The 3:1 rule is a guideline for microphone placement that can help to reduce the likelihood of phase cancellations and the comb filtering effect. The rule states that the distance between two microphones should be at least three times the distance between each microphone and the source of sound.

For example, if you're placing a microphone 1 foot away from a singer, the other microphone should be at least 3 feet away from both the singer and the first microphone. This distance helps to reduce the likelihood of phase cancellations and the comb filtering effect, which can occur when two microphones are picking up sound from the same source at different levels or times.

By following the 3:1 rule, you can help to ensure that the sound captured by each microphone is as distinct as possible, reducing the likelihood of phase cancellations and the comb filtering effect. Keep in mind that this is just a general guideline, and the best microphone placement will vary depending on the specific situation.

The Haas-Effect

This one's not as important in live environments, but if one of your inputs sound "thin" in the mix, and you want to make it sound like it's coming from a wider space, introduce a ~30ms delay in one of your stereo channels to make it sound wider. For Mono mixes, the Haas effect is a bit harder to pull off, but you can always duplicate the channel and pan one right, and the other left, and introduce a delay. You might have to tweak the timing so you don't get weird "chorus-like" artifacts or strange phase interactions.

Spectral-Widening

High-frequencies should be panned "wider" than lower frequencies because higher frequencies are more easily blocked by the head, which is why wide panned lower frequencies sound weird when wearing headphones.

Saturation for Translation

If you're struggling to find a way to get an instrument that don't have higher frequencies such as a Bass guitar to sound louder, try adding saturation to the lower-frequency sounds to create higher-frequency harmonics. This helps it sound fuller and more defined especially if the speakers are having a hard time replicating that low-frequency of a sound.

Make Reverb and Delay sound Natural

Toss an EQ on your reverb and delay effects and trim out the higher ends to simulate the loss of higher frequencies as sound travels further. It's a subtle, organic, and ethically-sourced way to make your mixes sound more believable.

Masking Sounds

It's pretty simple to understand that LOUD sounds make small sounds disappear. Mixing is a delicate balance to make every instrument and vocalist audible and blend together seamlessly. It's simple to just "turn up" the instrument that's quiet, but sometimes, it needs to be more subtle, sometimes you can just scoop out the frequencies of the acoustic guitar that's already covered by the electric guitar. You can just carve out frequency for different instruments to make each of them stand out.
